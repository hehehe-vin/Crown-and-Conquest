I have looked at this map so many times that Europe has stopped feeling like a continent and started feeling like an argument I intend to win. 
	The ink lines bleed into the parchment, mountain ranges reduced to mere scratches of charcoal, rivers turned into veins of fading blue. I trace the road to the east. My fingernail leaves a white groove in the paper, a ghost-path for eighty thousand men. Behind me, the Tuileries remains silent, a palace of stone and secrets. I can still feel the ghost of Josephine’s lips on my cheek. She kissed me goodbye this morning and then pulled back into a silence so profound it felt like a physical weight. She does that when she has decided not to say the thing she is thinking. I know what she is thinking. I am choosing, for now, not to know.
	Steam rises from the flanks of horses in the pre-dawn chill. The smell of the Grande Armée is a mixture of wet wool, gun oil, and the sharp, metallic tang of anticipation. 
	"The third wagon of maps, Sire."
	Berthier stands at my elbow. He hasn’t slept in three days; his eyes are mapped with red capillaries that mirror the roads of the Holy Roman Empire. He holds out a leather-bound case as if it contains the Host.
	"Seventeen copies, Berthier?" I ask, not looking up from the table. "You expect the wind to steal sixteen of them?"
	"I expect the world to be uncooperative, Sire," Berthier says, his voice a dry rasp. "One for the carriage, one for the saddlebags, five for the Marshals, and ten for the inevitable moment a courier drowns in a river that wasn't supposed to be there."
	"The rivers will be exactly where you put them on the page, Berthier. I don't pay you for topographical surprises."
	"I have also included the new weather reports from the Rhine. The mud is… persistent."
	"The mud is an opinion," I snap, finally looking at him. "My boots are a fact. Move the third corps to the flank by nightfall. If the mud argues, tell it I gave an order."
	Berthier bows, his ink-stained fingers twitching. He has written four years of letters to a woman in Munich that he has never sent. I see the edges of them peeking from his breast pocket. He lives in the details because the big picture—the sheer, terrifying scale of what we are doing—would crush a man of lesser precision.
	Outside the tent, the world is moving. Marshal Ney paws at the gravel with his boots as if he might strike sparks. He doesn't pace like a man waiting for a carriage; he paces the way a predator measures the bars of a cage. He sees me and stops, his hand instinctively finding the hilt of his saber.
	"The men are eating their shadows, Sire," Ney says. His voice is a battle-cry disguised as a greeting. "They want to know if we're marching to Vienna or just walking until the soles of their boots give out."
	"We are marching toward a sun that hasn't risen yet, Michel."
	"Give me the vanguard," Ney says, stepping closer. The scent of leather and unwashed skin follows him. "Let me break the crust of the Austrians before they've had their coffee. My horses are screaming for it."
	"You’ll have your glory, Ney. Try not to drown in it before I arrive."
	Ney grins, a flash of white teeth in a face bronzed by sun and scarred by the refusal to be ordinary. "Glory is a dry death, Sire. I prefer the wet kind, with a sword in my hand."
	He turns on his heel, already shouting orders to an aide-de-camp before his stride is finished. He believes in me the way other men believe in God—with a blind, frantic energy that masks a terror of being forgotten.
	Further down the line, Marshal Murat preens. He is a splash of impossible color against the grey morning—gold lace, crimson velvet, and a peacock feather in his hat that catches the first weak rays of light. He sits atop a white stallion that looks like it was carved from a cloud. 
	"A bit subtle for a campaign, isn't it, Joachim?" I call out.
	Murat adjusts his reins, his chest swelling. "A King should look like a King, Sire. Even if he hasn't found his kingdom yet."
	"Win the battle first. The crown comes later."
	"I am angling for both, Sire. Always."
	He rides off, a theatrical blur of vanity and genuine, reckless bravery. He wants a throne. Everyone here wants something they can’t name. 
	Talleyrand watches from the shadows of a black carriage, a pale, cold presence. He doesn't speak. He doesn't have to. His silence is a calculation, a reminder that he has served three regimes and is already measuring the weight of the fourth. I ignore him. To acknowledge Talleyrand is to acknowledge the possibility of an end.
	I walk to the edge of the rise. Below, the Grande Armée stretches out like a slumbering dragon. Eighty thousand men. I can hear the clatter of tin cups, the rhythmic thud of mallets, the low murmur of a thousand different dialects unified by a single purpose. They have decided to follow me into a continent. It does something to me—a tightening in the chest, a heat in the blood—that I will not name in any document that may be read later.
	The doubt is there, of course. It is a hairline crack in a marble column, thin as a hair and just as deep. I can feel it when I think of Josephine’s silence. I can feel it when I look at the vast, empty spaces on Berthier’s maps where the scouts haven't returned from. I am proving something to the world that the world does not know I am still trying to prove. That the boy from Corsica didn't just stumble into a crown. That I belong here, at the head of this dragon, holding the leash of history.
	I dismiss the doubt by moving faster than it can catch me.
	"Sire?" 
	It is a young lieutenant, his face pale, his uniform too clean. He holds my grey overcoat.
	"Is it time?" he asks.
	"It was time an hour ago," I say, snatching the coat and swinging it over my shoulders. The weight of it is a comfort. 
	I look toward the east. The horizon is a bruised purple, bleeding into orange. The first territory is ahead. Beyond that, the Austrians. Beyond that, the Russians. Beyond that, the judgment of time. 
	"Tell the drums to beat," I command. "I am very good at problems. I have been telling myself this since Corsica and it has been true every time. I see no reason today should be different."
