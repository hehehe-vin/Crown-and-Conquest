	The tallow candle gutters, casting a long, wavering shadow of my hand across the map of Prussia. Outside, the wind screams through the canvas of the command tent, a high, thin whistle that carries the scent of frozen mud and horse dung. 
	I pick up Ney’s dispatch. The paper is stiff with dried sleet. His handwriting, usually a frantic scrawl of loops and slashes, is uncharacteristically precise. He took the city of Magdeburg three days ago. I had ordered him to wait for the heavy artillery, to settle for a siege, to preserve his strength. He ignored me. He struck at dawn with half the allocated corps, bypassed the main gate, and turned the eastern wall before the sun cleared the horizon.
	Eleven hundred casualties were projected. He lost four hundred.
	"He wants me to see the ghost of the seven hundred men he saved," I mutter.
	Berthier looks up from his own table, his spectacles reflecting the candle flame like two dull coins. He has been marking the supply routes for six hours. He does not stop his pen. "The men are calling him the 'Bravest of the Brave' again, Sire. It is a popular sentiment."
	"It is a dangerous sentiment when it replaces 'The Most Obedient,'" I say. "He didn't win by instinct, Berthier. Look at the logistics of his flanking maneuver. He calculated the wind speed against the smoke of the initial volley. He made a decision to discard my plan because he believed his was better. And he was right."
	"The city is ours," Berthier says, his voice flat, drained of anything but data. "The road to Berlin is open."
	"And the road to my irrelevance is paved with such victories." I toss the dispatch onto the table. It lands near a cluster of letters. "What is this new ink on the quartermaster’s charts? This violet?"
	Berthier stiffens. He sets the pen down. He doesn't look at me; he looks at the map where the supply lines from the Rhine stretch thin, like frayed threads about to snap. 
	"A situation requiring monitoring, Sire," he says.
	"You use that tone for typhus and mutiny. Which is it?"
	"The local grain stores in the north are empty. The violet marks districts where the forage has failed completely. We are feeding the horses the thatch from the peasants' roofs. If the snow thickens, the violet will spread."
	"Manage it," I say. "Move the bread wagons from the third reserve."
	"The third reserve is currently being redirected to the new duchy," Berthier says.
	"What new duchy?"
	Berthier slides a heavy, cream-colored envelope toward me. It bears the seal of Marshal Murat. I break the wax. The signature at the bottom is written in a flourish that takes up nearly a third of the page: *His Royal Highness, Joachim.*
	"He has no crown," I say, the words tasting like copper. "I have not given him a kingdom. I have not even promised him a province."
	"He is already holding court in the captured villas," Berthier says. "He has commissioned a new uniform. White velvet with gold egret feathers. He told the locals he is the vanguard of a new royalty."
	"The man is a peacock who thinks the sun rises to hear him crow." I drop the letter as if it’s scorched. "He is performing the role before the theater has been built. If I stop him, I lose my best cavalry commander. If I let him continue, I have a king who owes his throne to his own vanity rather than my hand."
	"And then there is Vienna," Berthier adds, pointing to a smaller, more discreet note tucked under my compass.
	Talleyrand. The man’s script is like a spider’s web—elegant, thin, and designed to trap. He has negotiated a separate peace with the Austrian envoys regarding the Italian border. He had no authority to speak for the Empire. He went behind my back while I was counting boots in the mud.
	I read the terms. My eyes ache. "The fool," I whisper.
	"Is the deal poor, Sire?"
	"No," I snap. "It is perfect. It secures the southern flank, stabilizes the currency, and provides a buffer that would have taken me three months of campaigning to achieve. It is a masterpiece of diplomacy."
	"Then why the 'fool'?"
	"Because he did it without me. Because now the Austrians know they can talk to the Minister when the Emperor is being difficult. Success that bypasses the throne is a subtle form of treason, Berthier. It’s a rot that smells like roses."
	I stand and pace the length of the tent. My boots crunch on the frozen ground. I feel the machine of the Grande Armée vibrating beneath my feet. It is a magnificent engine, polished and lethal, but the gears are beginning to turn at different speeds. Ney is winning his own wars. Murat is dreaming of his own palaces. Talleyrand is ruling his own Europe. 
	I am the architect, but they are beginning to believe they are the owners.
	I stop at the small writing desk in the corner. A letter from Josephine sits there, the violet ribbon already untied. I know the contents. It is warm. She speaks of the gardens at Malmaison, of the new roses. But it is four paragraphs shorter than the one she sent from Paris. The space between her words is growing, a silent map of its own.
	"Sire?" Berthier asks. "The orders for tomorrow? Do we reprimand Ney for the unauthorized assault?"
	I look at the map. The violet ink of the forage failure stares back at me like a bruise on the face of the continent. If I punish Ney, the army will resent me for stifling a hero. If I reward him, I signal that my orders are merely suggestions. 
	I pick up a pen. The nib scratches against the parchment, a harsh, rhythmic sound in the silence of the tent. 
	"Write to Ney," I say. "Tell him his report was received. Tell him I have noted his 'decision.' Do not mention the city. Do not mention the casualties."
	"And the promotion he expects?"
	"Tell him he is to hold Magdeburg until I arrive. He is not to move a single picket line without my written seal. If he breathes without my permission, I will strip him of his command."
	"He will be insulted," Berthier says.
	"He will be reminded," I correct him. "And send a dispatch to Murat. Address it to 'General Murat.' Use the title three times in the first paragraph. If he uses 'Royal Highness' again, I will have his egret feathers plucked and his white velvet used to patch the horses' blankets."
	I pause, looking at Talleyrand’s deal. My thumb brushes the elegant script. I could tear it up. I could ruin the peace just to prove I can. But the violet on the map—the hunger, the cold—it demands the deal stay.
	"Accept Talleyrand’s terms," I say, the words feeling heavy in my throat. "But send the confirmation through my private office, not his. I want the Austrians to think he was merely my messenger, not the author."
	Berthier nods, his pen flying across the paper. "A complex series of maneuvers, Sire."
	"It is no longer a campaign, Berthier. It is an equilibrium." 
	I walk to the tent flap and pull it back. The winter air hits me like a blow, sharp and smelling of woodsmoke and the iron tang of the coming snow. Down the hill, a thousand campfires flicker in the dark, a galaxy of orange sparks against the black Prussian night. Each fire represents men who belong to me, yet the space between this tent and those fires feels wider than it did yesterday. 
	I am at the center of everything, and for the first time, I can feel the center starting to pull. 
	"Prepare the carriage for dawn," I say, not looking back. "I want to be at the front. I need to see the city Ney took. I need to see if the gates look different when they are won without my word."
	The wind catches the map on the table, fluttering the edges. The violet ink looks darker in the fading light, almost black, like a stain that won’t come out. I drop the tent flap, sealing myself back into the warmth and the lies, and begin to plan a victory that will belong only to me.
