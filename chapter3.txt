	The portrait above the fireplace belongs to a Duke whose name I have already forgotten. He stares down with the vacant confidence of a man who never expected to lose his house to a Corsican. The room is vast, a cavern of Gilded Age marble and drafty corners, yet the air remains stubborn. It smells of damp stone and the stale wax of a thousand candles.
	I smooth the letter across the map of the central territories. Seven nations are pinned beneath my palms, their borders redrawn in French ink. 
	Josephine’s handwriting flows with a heat that usually warms my chest. This time, the heat feels like a fever. I have read the parchment four times. She writes of the east garden in Paris. She writes of the roses, claims they have bloomed in a shade somewhere between the red of a soldier's coat and the purple-grey of a sky before a storm. 
	I close my eyes and reach for the garden. I find nothing. 
	I can recall the angle of the sun at Marengo. I can recite the elevation of the heights at Austerlitz. But the garden where I once walked with her is a blur of grey. The more I reach for it, the faster the image dissolves into the grit and smoke of the march.
	A rhythmic tap sounds at the door. Berthier enters before I can grant leave. He carries a fresh roll of vellum and a pot of blue pigment. He moves with the mechanical grace of a man who has replaced his soul with a ledger.
	"The eastern lines, Sire," Berthier says. He spreads the map over Josephine’s letter. "A situation requiring monitoring."
	"You used that phrase yesterday, Berthier. And the day before."
	"The supply wagons are sinking into the mud of the spring thaw. The horses eat the thatch off the roofs of the villages. We are winning, but the machine is grinding its own gears."
	I look at the new blue lines he has drawn. They look like veins on a cold hand. 
	"Ney fought at Guttstadt," I say. I do not look up. "He moved without my signature."
	"He won, Sire. The Russians are in retreat toward the Alle."
	"He gambled with my center. If the Russians had held the ridge, Ney would be a corpse and my line would be a severed string."
	Berthier dips his quill. "He is waiting in the antechamber. He expects a laurel. Or a lashing."
	"He will wait longer. A man who wins without orders is more dangerous than a man who loses with them."
	Berthier pauses, his quill hovering over the border of a territory I haven't named yet. "Murat has sent a dispatch from Naples. He speaks of 'his' people now. He asks for a new shipment of silk for the palace guards."
	"The crown fits him too well," I mutter. "He forgets who forged the gold."
	"Talleyrand remains in the wings, Sire. He is whispering to the Austrian envoy again. He says peace is a fruit that rots if left on the branch too long."
	"Talleyrand is a tongue looking for a mouth to inhabit. Tell him I am not hungry for his fruit."
	I shove the map aside. The letter is still there, crumpled slightly at the corner. The roses. The sky before a storm. I can smell the iron in the air of this room, the scent of a coming rain, but I cannot see the flowers. 
	"Josephine did not ask when I am returning," I say.
	Berthier stops. He looks at me, his face a mask of professional neutrality. "The Empress understands the gravity of the campaign, Sire."
	"She always asks, Berthier. Every letter for ten years. 'When will the wind blow you back to me?' or 'The bed is a cold continent without you.' This time? Roses. Soil. The weather in the east garden."
	"Perhaps she is being practical. You are the master of Europe. Practicality is the language of the age."
	"Practicality is the language of people who have stopped waiting."
	I stand and walk to the window. The courtyard below is a hive of blue coats and steel. Men are sharpening bayonets against whetstones. The sound is a high, thin scream that never ends. Beyond the gates, the road leads to the next city, and the city after that.
	"The next map, Sire," Berthier prompts. "The advance on Königsberg."
	I hear the spurs of Marshal Ney clicking in the hall. He enters without the hesitation of a subordinate. He smells of horse sweat and black powder. His face is flushed with the high of a victory he stole from the gods of discipline.
	"The Russians are broken, Sire," Ney says. His voice booms, too loud for this cold room. "I took the bridgehead. They ran like scalded dogs."
	I turn from the window. I look at the man who could have destroyed my campaign with a single impulse. I have the words of a court-martial in my throat. I can feel the weight of them—the logic, the necessity of order. If I do not break him now, the Marshals will become Kings before I am finished with them.
	"You risked ten thousand men on a whim, Michel," I say.
	"I risked them on a certainty, Sire. I saw the gap. I took it."
	"There are no gaps I do not see first."
	Ney steps closer to the table. He looks at the maps, then at the letter I have failed to hide. "The army is tired, Napoleon. They want to know what we are chasing. We have the land. We have the glory."
	"We have the momentum," I snap. "The moment we sit, we sink."
	"And the Empress? Does she want momentum?"
	I feel the cold of the palace floor seeping through my boots. "The Empress writes of her garden. She does not concern herself with bridgeheads."
	"Then she is the only one of us with any sense," Ney says. He doesn't wait for a dismissal. He turns on his heel and clatters out, leaving the smell of the battlefield behind.
	I look at Berthier. He is waiting with the blue ink.
	"Is the color correct, Berthier?"
	"The map, Sire?"
	"The roses. Between red and the sky before a storm. Is that a color a man can actually see?"
	Berthier blinks. "I... I couldn't say, Sire. I deal in terrain and tonnage."
	"Of course you do. We all do."
	I sit back down at the desk. My hands are steady—the hands of a man who has built seven nations in the time it takes most men to lose a skirmish. I am brilliant. I am in command. My mind is a clockwork of logistics and grand strategy.
	But I am staring at the map because I am afraid to look at the letter again. 
	If I read it a fifth time, the absence of the question will become a permanent silence. If she has stopped asking when I am coming home, it is because she has realized there is no version of me left to return. The man who walked in the garden is a ghost that even I can no longer conjure.
	"The next city," I say, my voice sounding like gravel.
	Berthier unrolls the final sheet. "Königsberg, Sire. The gateway to the north."
	"Mark it."
	I pick up the compasses. I fix the needle in the heart of a city I have never seen. I lean over the table, my eyes tracing the roads, the rivers, the elevations. I focus on the ink. I focus on the distance. I focus on the map until the memory of a rose is nothing but a smudge of red in a world that is turning to winter.
