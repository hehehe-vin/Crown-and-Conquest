	The indigo ink of the Empire had not yet dried on the tenth province. It spread across the vellum like a slow, deliberate bruise, marking where my boots had trod and where my cannons had sung. I stood over the map table in a room that smelled of cold wax and the damp stone of a conquered palace. The ceiling was too high, the gilding too bright, the air too still. It was a room built for a man who didn't exist anymore.
	Berthier stood by the heavy oak doors, his uniform buttons catching the pale autumn light. He held the morning report like a votive offering.
	"The munitions count from the third corps, Sire," Berthier said. His voice was a flat, familiar percussion. "And the logistics for the crossing. Everything is exactly as you ordered."
	I took the papers. I didn't look at them. I knew the numbers would be perfect; Berthier was a clockwork man.
	"Is there a discrepancy?" he asked, his posture shifting just a fraction.
	"No, Berthier. It is correct."
	I waited for the rest of the sentence to leave my throat—the 'thank you,' the 'well done'—but it died behind my teeth. I had not said those words to him in three campaigns. I watched the way his hand hovered near his sword hilt, waiting for a dismissal that was as routine as the sunrise. I was beginning to notice the things I almost never did.
	"Leave it," I said.
	The doors clicked shut. The silence that followed was louder than the drums of the Grande Armée. It was the same silence Talleyrand had left in my wake this morning, a space shaped like a question I didn't want to answer.
	We had stood by the tall windows overlooking the courtyard. Talleyrand had leaned on his cane, his eyes as unreadable as a frozen pond.
	"Madrid will fall, of course," Talleyrand had said, his tone suggesting the city was already a ghost. "The locals have a taste for martyrdom, but your brother has a taste for thrones. It is a messy combination, but a predictable one."
	"And the British?" I had asked.
	"A nuisance. Like dust in a watch. They slow the gears, but they do not stop them."
	He had paused then, turning his head just enough so the light caught the edge of his powdered wig.
	"It is Ney who concerns me," he murmured.
	"Ney is my bravest," I countered. "He would charge the gates of hell if I gave him a horse and a whistle."
	"Exactly," Talleyrand said. "He is the most dangerous kind of soldier."
	"Because he does not fear death?"
	"No, Sire. Because Ney’s bravery is not about France. And it is not about the campaign."
	He had stopped there. He didn't need to finish. The silence hung between us, a jagged thing. I knew what lay at the end of that thought. Ney’s bravery was about the void inside him—the need to be the last man standing in a world he had helped burn down. He wasn't fighting for me. He was fighting for the moment the bullet finally found a way to stop his heart. I had been carrying that unfinished sentence all day, a lead weight in my pocket.
	I turned back to the desk. Murat’s dispatch sat atop a pile of leather-bound ledgers. It was thick, heavy paper, smelling of Mediterranean salt and expensive musk. I broke the seal.
	*To My Brother-in-Arms and Sovereign,* it began.
	The text was a masterpiece of royal etiquette, a dance of words that never quite touched the ground. He signed it with a flourish that took up half the page: *Joachim Napoleon, King of Naples.*
	I traced the word *King* with my thumb. I had placed that gold circle on his head. I had given him the kingdom, the title, the very name he now used to address me as an equal. I looked at the map of the ten territories and wondered how many more kings I would have to manufacture before I ran out of friends. I was surrounded by the ghosts of the men I had made, and they all spoke to me in the voices of monarchs.
	The smallest scrap of paper on the desk was Josephine’s letter. 
	It sat near the inkwell, the wax seal a deep, blood-red. I had reached for it four times since the sun hit the meridian. Each time, something else had intervened. A courier from the vanguard. A dispute over the grain prices in Warsaw. The next thing. There was always a next thing, a relentless tide of trivialities that demanded the Emperor’s hand so the man could remain buried.
	I picked it up. I could almost feel the heat of her skin in the paper, the scent of the roses she wrote about—the ones in the east garden I could no longer visualize. I tried to see the path, the way the light hit the fountain, but all I saw were lines of march and the topography of a ridge near Madrid.
	A sharp knock echoed through the room.
	"Sire?" It was a young aide-de-camp, breathless and bright-eyed. "The engineers have reached the river. They require your signature on the bridge schematics before the moon rises. The Marshal says it cannot wait."
	I looked at the letter. The seal was still unbroken. If I opened it, I would have to be the man who missed the garden. If I put it down, I remained the man who moved the world.
	"The schematics," I said, my voice sounding like gravel grinding together.
	"Yes, Sire. They are in the anteroom."
	I laid the letter back on the blotter, perfectly aligned with the edge of the desk. I did not look back as I walked toward the door. The high ceilings swallowed the sound of my boots.
	"Tell the engineers I am coming," I said.
	The next thing had arrived. It was always more comfortable than the last.
