	The frost bit at the canvas of the tent, a rhythmic snapping that mimicked the crackle of the dying fire. Berthier’s shadow stretched across the final map of Europe, his quill hovering over the last two slivers of white territory.
	"The Russian rearguard has folded into the tree line at the edge of the final province, Sire," Berthier said, his voice a dry parchment scratch. "The 3rd Corps is positioned. We await only the word."
	I looked at him, really looked at him, for the first time in years. "The report you filed an hour ago, Louis-Alexandre. It was correct. In every detail."
	Berthier froze. The quill dripped a single glob of ink onto the edge of the world. "I... thank you, Sire. It is as it must be."
	"It has always been correct," I said, the words feeling heavy, like coins being paid toward an old debt. "I should have told you that. Often."
	He didn't look up, but his shoulders dropped an inch. "The maps do not care for praise, Sire. Only for accuracy."
	The flap of the tent pulled back, admitting a swirl of ice and the scent of damp wool. Ney stood there, his red hair a chaotic torch against the winter night. He looked like a man who had forgotten what a house felt like.
	"The men have sharpened their steel, Bonaparte," Ney said, his voice a low growl. "They’re shivering, but they’ve stopped complaining. That’s when you know they’re ready to kill. They want the end of the world tonight."
	"Is the morale holding?" I asked.
	"They talk about Paris," Ney said, stepping closer to the heat. "They talk about bread and women and the way the sun feels in June. They don't talk about the empire anymore. They just want to finish the map so they can walk home."
	"Then we give them the horizon," I said. "Move the cavalry to the eastern flank. We end this before the sun reaches its zenith."
	"And if the Tsar sues for peace before the first charge?" Ney asked, a crooked grin touching his scarred face.
	"He won't," I said. "He has nowhere left to run. This is the final reckoning we both promised ourselves."
	"I’ll see you at the summit, then," Ney said, nodding once before vanishing back into the dark.
	I turned back to the table. The silence of the tent felt immense, a physical weight that pressed against my lungs.
	"Will you be joining the vanguard, Sire?" Berthier asked, his hand steadying the map.
	"No," I said, reaching into my coat. My fingers brushed the crisp edge of the letter. "I have something to finish here first."
	"The final orders?"
	"A final truth," I replied. I pulled the sealed envelope out. "Berthier, find a courier. One with the fastest horse in the stable."
	"The lines are still contested, Sire. It may not pass through until the morning."
	"Then he waits at the edge of the smoke," I said. "As soon as the last flag falls, he rides for Paris. Not for the Senate. Not for the palace. To the house in the Rue de la Victoire."
	"Understood," Berthier said, taking the letter as if it were made of glass. 
	I walked to the tent opening. The horizon was beginning to grey, the silhouettes of fifty thousand men rising like ghosts from the frozen earth. The sound of a distant trumpet cut through the cold, sharp and final.
	I closed my eyes and didn't see the lines of infantry. I saw the east garden. I heard the sound of her laugh—ordinary, specific, and loud enough to drown out the coming guns.
	"Is it worth it, Sire?" Berthier’s voice came from the shadows behind me. "The whole map?"
	"The map is just paper, Berthier," I said, watching the first flash of cannon fire light up the sky. "The laugh is the only thing that was ever real."
